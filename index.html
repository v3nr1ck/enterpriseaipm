The thesis

A PRD is an opinion with formatting. So is a roadmap deck. For twenty years that was fine, because opinions were the best artifact available — building the real thing was expensive, so organizations argued about descriptions of the thing instead. Review cycles exist because a document can’t be tested. It can only be believed, and belief takes meetings.

AI changed exactly one variable: the cost of a working artifact. A product manager can now answer a feasibility debate with a deployed prototype, a data question with a pipeline you can query, a discovery question with software the engineers can poke at — in less time than it takes to get the debate onto someone’s calendar. I’ve measured this on my own work. Discovery-to-PRD went from roughly two weeks to roughly 48 hours, and I wasn’t the one doing the measuring.

Here’s the underlying system. The document was never the product; it was a proxy for a thing too expensive to build speculatively. Organizations optimize proxies — they can’t help it, proxies are what the incentives can see. But when the real thing gets cheaper than the proxy for the thing, the proxy starts dying. Slowly, because the incentives keep pointing at it long after everyone involved knows better. The direction isn’t in question, though.

Which sets up the failure mode I keep watching enterprises walk into: treating this as a procurement problem. Buy every PM a license, run a training, declare victory. What you get is AI-assisted PMs — the same documents, produced faster. Faster proxies. The organization needed fewer proxies, and it bought a proxy accelerator.

Getting AI-native PMs means changing the system the PMs operate in: a shared definition of what good looks like at each level, guardrails that make speed safe to use, a place where one PM’s tooling becomes everyone’s tooling, and measurement honest enough to catch theater. An operating model. The rest of this page is the one I run.

(Some documents deserve to live, to be clear. Strategy memos and decision records are thinking, and thinking doesn’t have a prototype. The documents that die are the ones that were standing in for working software.)

The AI-native PM competency ladder

Five levels, defined by what you can observe — behaviors and artifacts, not tool familiarity, which mostly measures enthusiasm. The test at every level is the same: what does this person’s work change for other people?

  1. 1

    Assisted

    Uses AI as a faster keyboard

    Behaviors
    Drafting, summarizing, rewriting. Chat as a convenience layer on top of an unchanged workflow.
    Artifacts
    The same documents, written faster and slightly better.
    Organizational signal
    None. This is where most organizations are today, and where most training programs stop.
  2. 2

    Fluent

    Has patterns, not just prompts

    Behaviors
    Reusable prompt patterns, multi-document synthesis, deliberate model selection. Treats context as something you engineer rather than something you hope for.
    Artifacts
    Research briefs distilled from corpora nobody had time to read; competitive teardowns; analysis that used to be a week of open tabs.
    Organizational signal
    Cycle time visibly drops — for one person. Peers start asking how.
  3. 3

    Builder

    Answers questions with working software

    Behaviors
    Builds retrieval over real document sets, functional prototypes, scrapers, dashboards, small internal tools. Ships things engineering can evaluate instead of interpret.
    Artifacts
    Deployed prototypes. Queryable pipelines. Discovery output you can click on.
    Organizational signal
    Engineering starts pulling PM prototypes into evaluation instead of translating PM documents. Feasibility arguments get shorter, because there’s less left to argue about.
  4. 4

    Orchestrator

    Runs systems of agents; publishes tools others adopt

    Behaviors
    Designs multi-agent workflows with defined roles, persistent memory, and review built in. Packages personal tooling so colleagues can install it rather than admire it.
    Artifacts
    Published agents and skills in a shared internal marketplace; pipelines that run without their author in the room.
    Organizational signal
    Other people’s work gets faster. First level where the gains leave the individual — and it comes with a number you can check: installs.
  5. 5

    Multiplier

    Changes what the discipline expects of itself

    Behaviors
    Designs the operating model itself: competency definitions wired into career frameworks, gates wired into intake, shared tooling, measurement that survives a skeptical audience.
    Artifacts
    Standards, maturity assessments, governance rails, curricula — and org-level cycle-time numbers with baselines attached.
    Organizational signal
    The gains persist if the individual leaves. That’s the test.
The distribution problem: most enterprises today have many 1s, some 2s, a few 3s (usually unrecognized, usually solving their own problems quietly), and approximately zero 4s and 5s. Not because the skills are rare — because levels 4 and 5 aren’t individual skills at all. They’re organizational permissions. Nobody reaches level 5 by being personally productive; someone with authority has to let them change what the PM job is defined as. Which makes this an org-design decision wearing a training program’s clothes.

Seven responsible-AI gates

Speed without governance is how AI programs die in legal review — or worse, in production. These are the seven questions I make a product decision answer before AI ships in it. They’re written as gates rather than principles for a simple reason: you can’t fail a principle.

  1. Grounding. Can every generated answer cite its source? A system that can’t show where a claim came from isn’t ready for users whose decisions cost money.
  2. Data boundary. Which data reaches which model, under what agreement, retained for how long? “It’s just a prompt” is how confidential data leaves companies.
  3. Human accountability. A named human owns every AI output that reaches a customer or a decision. If nobody will put their name on it, it doesn’t ship.
  4. Failure design. What does a wrong answer cost, who catches it, and what’s the fallback? The failure path gets designed with the same rigor as the demo path — before the incident, which is the cheap time to do it.
  5. Accessibility and inclusion. AI experiences meet WCAG 2.2 AA and work with assistive technology in the first release, not the retrofit. Excluded users don’t file bug reports. They leave.
  6. Measurement. A baseline recorded before launch, and a metric that could show failure — not just success. Enthusiasm is not an instrument.
  7. Disclosure. People know when they’re reading machine output and when a decision about them involved a model. They find out eventually either way; the only real choice is whether you told them first.
Why gates instead of principles?

Principles describe intent. Gates sit in the intake template and the launch checklist, where the work actually flows, and they block things. A principle survives being ignored — that’s roughly its defining feature. The test of a governance model isn’t whether it reads well; it’s whether a team can fail it. This one can.

The 90-day adoption playbook

How to move a PM organization up the ladder without a reorg, a platform purchase, or a mandate nobody follows. Order matters: measure, then build the commons, then wire it into promotions. Most programs run this backwards, which is why most programs are slide decks.

Days 0–30

Baseline & seed

  • Instrument current cycle times: discovery-to-PRD, PRD-to-decision, question-to-answer. You can’t claim gains you never baselined, and someone will ask.
  • Map the org against the ladder, honestly. Expect a pyramid.
  • Find the level-3s already hiding in the org — every org has a few, quietly solving their own problems because nobody sanctioned it. They’re the seed cohort.
  • Stand up a sanctioned sandbox with explicit data-boundary rules, so experimentation stops being shadow IT.

Days 31–60

Build the commons

  • Launch an internal marketplace where PM-built agents, skills, and templates are installable by anyone. This is the mechanism that turns one level-4 into a hundred faster PMs.
  • Put the seven gates directly into intake and PRD templates — governance as a form field, not a memo.
  • Seed-cohort office hours on real backlog items. Demos on toy problems convince nobody.
  • Publish the first before/after cycle-time numbers internally. Evidence recruits volunteers; mandates recruit compliance.

Days 61–90

Wire it into promotions

  • Put the ladder into the PM career framework. Promotion criteria are the only adoption mechanism an enterprise has that survives leadership turnover.
  • Stand up a lightweight review board for gate exceptions, so governance has an owner and a queue instead of a vibe.
  • Report org-level numbers to leadership: cycle-time deltas, marketplace installs, gate pass rates.
  • Rotate seed-cohort members into every product family as named practice leads.

Receipts

An operating model is worth exactly as much as its author’s practice. Mine, briefly — generalized where the details belong to my employer rather than to me.

  • Production AI, shipped. Wrote the first-version RAG engine behind an assistant that field technicians across 4,700+ retail locations now use daily — thousands of technical manuals ingested, chunked, embedded, and served with cited answers. Engineering took the prototype into the official build, which is the only compliment from engineering that counts.
  • Tools other PMs install. Three agents and skills authored and published to my company’s internal AI marketplace — a notes-to-prototype pipeline and an automated design-standards auditor among them. Installable by anyone in the company, which means the level-4 claim comes with a number attached.
  • Enterprise-scale results. Digital-twin and predictive-analytics platform tied to $1.5B+ in measured GMV impact. IoT monitoring contributing to $1B+ in annual waste reduction. A work-order platform serving 1.2M associates. Earlier careers: cloud-native 5G core delivery, and three communications spacecraft from vendor selection to on-orbit commissioning.
  • Audited velocity. Discovery-to-PRD compressed from roughly two weeks to roughly 48 hours. The figure isn’t mine — it came from independent AI audits of six months of my own session logs. Gate #6 applies to me too.
  • Personal practice, personally funded. 300+ fine-tuned models across image, video, and mixture-of-experts LLMs. 100B+ parameter open-weight models running on my own hardware. Custom multi-agent orchestration with cost-aware routing between local and paid inference. Fifty published browser extensions, fifty public model-hub repos. When I make a call at work about hosting tradeoffs or fine-tuning feasibility, it’s because I already made it at home, on my own dime.
Why the details are generalized

Some of this concerns internal systems and figures that belong to my employer, not to me. Gate #2 doesn’t stop applying just because the data would make me look good. What’s here is what I’d defend in front of the people who own it.

Accessibility statement

This site is built to WCAG 2.2 Level AA. Partly because accessibility is a competence I think belongs in the PM skill set, and partly because failing gate #5 on my own homepage would be a bad look.

  • Semantic HTML landmarks and heading hierarchy throughout; a skip-to-content link is the first focusable element.
  • All text meets or exceeds a 4.5:1 contrast ratio in both light and dark themes (the page follows your system preference).
  • Fully keyboard-operable; visible focus indicators with 3px outlines and offset, never obscured by sticky elements.
  • Honors prefers-reduced-motion; no animation, autoplay, or parasitic movement.
  • No JavaScript required — the page is fully functional with scripts disabled. Disclosure widgets use native, accessible elements.
  • Text scales to 200% without loss of content or function; layout is responsive to 320px viewports.
  • No external resources: no CDN scripts, no third-party fonts, no trackers, no cookies.

If you hit an accessibility barrier on this page, email me and I’ll fix it. That’s how gates work: they apply to the person who wrote them.
