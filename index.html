The thesis

For twenty years, the unit of product management work has been the document: the PRD, the roadmap deck, the business case. Documents are opinions with formatting. Organizations learned to route them through review cycles precisely because opinions need consensus to become action.

AI collapsed the cost of a different unit of work: the working artifact. A product manager can now answer a discovery question with a functioning prototype, a data question with a queryable pipeline, and a feasibility debate with a deployed demo — in the time it used to take to schedule the meeting. When evidence gets that cheap, opinion-based artifacts stop being competitive. The PMs who internalize this compress discovery-to-decision cycles from weeks to days. I have watched it happen, measured it, and been the subject of the measurement.

Here is the uncomfortable part for large organizations: this advantage does not scale by procurement. Buying every PM a license produces AI-assisted PMs — the same documents, faster. Producing AI-native PMs requires an operating model: a shared definition of what good looks like at each level, guardrails that make speed safe, an enablement layer where one PM’s tooling becomes everyone’s tooling, and measurement that separates real cycle-time gains from theater.

That operating model is what this page contains. It is not a manifesto; it is the system I run, offered in a form another organization could adopt on Monday.

The AI-native PM competency ladder

Five levels, each defined by observable behaviors and artifacts — not tool familiarity. The question at every level is the same: what does this person’s work make possible for other people?

  1. 1

    Assisted

    Uses AI as a faster keyboard

    Behaviors
    Drafting, summarizing, rewriting. Chat as a convenience layer over existing workflow.
    Artifacts
    Better-written documents, produced somewhat faster.
    Organizational signal
    Individual convenience. Nothing changes for anyone else.
  2. 2

    Fluent

    Has patterns, not just prompts

    Behaviors
    Reusable prompt patterns; multi-document synthesis; deliberate model selection; treats context as something you engineer, not something you hope for.
    Artifacts
    Research briefs from large corpora, competitive teardowns, structured analyses that would previously have been a week of reading.
    Organizational signal
    Individual cycle time visibly drops. Peers start asking “how did you do that so fast?”
  3. 3

    Builder

    Answers questions with working software

    Behaviors
    Builds retrieval pipelines over real document sets, functional prototypes, scrapers, dashboards, and internal utilities. Ships things engineering can evaluate rather than interpret.
    Artifacts
    Deployed prototypes; data pipelines; tools that remove personal friction. Discovery output is runnable.
    Organizational signal
    Engineering starts pulling PM prototypes into evaluation instead of translating PM documents. The discovery-to-decision cycle compresses by an order of magnitude.
  4. 4

    Orchestrator

    Runs systems of agents; publishes tools others adopt

    Behaviors
    Designs multi-agent workflows with defined roles, persistent memory, and quality review built in. Packages personal tooling so colleagues can install it, not admire it.
    Artifacts
    Published agents and skills in a shared internal marketplace; pipelines that run without their author present.
    Organizational signal
    Other people’s work gets faster. This is the first level where the gains leave the individual.
  5. 5

    Multiplier

    Changes what the discipline expects of itself

    Behaviors
    Designs the operating model: competency definitions wired into career frameworks, responsible-AI governance wired into intake, enablement infrastructure, and measurement that survives scrutiny.
    Artifacts
    Standards, maturity assessments, governance rails, training curricula — and measured org-level cycle-time and quality gains.
    Organizational signal
    The gains persist if the individual leaves. The discipline itself has changed.
The distribution problem: most enterprises today have many L1s, some L2s, a handful of L3s, and almost no L4/L5 — because levels 4 and 5 are not individual skills. They require someone with the standing to change how the organization defines the PM job. That is an organizational design decision, and it is the highest-leverage one available to a product org right now.

Seven responsible-AI gates

Speed without governance is how AI programs die in legal review — or worse, in production. These are the seven questions I require a product decision involving AI to answer. They are written as gates, not values statements, because values statements don’t block a launch.

  1. Grounding. Can every AI-generated answer cite its source? If the system cannot show where a claim came from, it is not ready for users whose decisions carry cost.
  2. Data boundary. Exactly which data reaches which model, under what agreement, with what retention? “It’s just a prompt” is how confidential data leaves a company.
  3. Human accountability. A named human owns every AI output that reaches a customer or a decision. If no one will sign their name to it, it does not ship.
  4. Failure design. What does a wrong answer cost, who catches it, and what is the fallback? Design the failure path with the same rigor as the happy path — before launch, not after the incident.
  5. Accessibility and inclusion. AI-powered experiences meet WCAG 2.2 AA and work with assistive technology from the first release. Accessibility retrofits cost more than accessibility defaults, and excluded users don’t file bug reports — they leave.
  6. Measurement. A pre-registered baseline and a metric that would show failure, not only success. If the only evidence is enthusiasm, the gate stays closed.
  7. Disclosure. People know when they are reading machine-generated content and when a decision about them involved a model. Trust is a product surface.
Why gates instead of principles?

Principles describe intent; gates change behavior. A principle survives being ignored — a gate does not, because it sits in the intake template, the PRD skeleton, and the launch checklist where work actually flows. The test of an AI governance model is not whether it reads well but whether a team can fail it. This one can be failed, which is why passing it means something.

The 90-day adoption playbook

How to move a PM organization up the ladder without a reorg, a platform purchase, or a mandate nobody follows. The sequence matters: measure, then enable, then institutionalize.

Days 0–30

Baseline & seed

  • Instrument current artifact cycle times: discovery-to-PRD, PRD-to-decision, question-to-answer. You cannot claim gains you never baselined.
  • Map the org against the ladder honestly. Expect a pyramid.
  • Identify the existing L3+ practitioners — every org has a few, usually unrecognized — and make them the seed cohort.
  • Stand up a sanctioned sandbox with clear data-boundary rules, so experimentation stops being shadow IT.

Days 31–60

Build the enablement layer

  • Launch an internal marketplace where PM-built agents, skills, and templates are installable by anyone — the mechanism that turns L4 individuals into org-wide gains.
  • Embed the seven gates directly into intake and PRD templates.
  • Seed-cohort office hours: working sessions on real backlog items, not training-slide demos.
  • Publish the first before/after cycle-time numbers internally. Evidence recruits better than mandates.

Days 61–90

Institutionalize

  • Wire the ladder into the PM career framework — promotion criteria are the only durable adoption mechanism an enterprise has.
  • Stand up a lightweight review board for gate exceptions, so governance has an owner.
  • Report org-level metrics to leadership: cycle-time deltas, marketplace adoption, gate pass rates.
  • Rotate seed-cohort members into every product family as named practice leads.

Receipts

An operating model is only as credible as its author’s practice. Mine, briefly — details generalized where they concern internal systems.

  • Production AI, shipped. Authored the first-version retrieval-augmented (RAG) engine behind an AI assistant now used daily by field technicians across 4,700+ retail locations — thousands of technical manuals ingested, chunked, embedded, and served with cited answers. Engineering adopted the prototype into the official build: the strongest technical-soundness vote a PM’s work can receive.
  • Tools other PMs install. Three agents/skills authored and published to my company’s internal AI marketplace, installable by any employee — a notes-to-prototype pipeline and an automated design-standards auditor among them. Level 4 is not a claim; it is a download count.
  • Enterprise-scale product results. Digital-twin and predictive-analytics platform tied to $1.5B+ in measured GMV impact; IoT monitoring platform contributing to $1B+ in annual waste reduction; work-order platform serving 1.2M associates. Earlier: cloud-native 5G core delivery and three communications spacecraft, concept to on-orbit commissioning.
  • Measured, independently audited velocity. Discovery-to-PRD cycle compressed from roughly two weeks to roughly 48 hours — a figure produced not by me but by independent AI audits of six months of my own session logs. I hold my own claims to gate #6.
  • Personal practice, personally funded. 300+ fine-tuned models (LoRA/QLoRA across image, video, and mixture-of-experts LLMs); 100B+ parameter open-weight models hosted on my own hardware; custom multi-agent orchestration with cost-aware routing; 50 published browser extensions and 50 public model-hub repositories. The judgment I bring to enterprise AI decisions is backed by hands-on practice I pay for myself.
Why the details are generalized

Some of this work concerns internal systems and figures that belong to my employer, not to me. Gate #2 — the data boundary — applies to career artifacts too. What I publish here is what I would be comfortable defending in front of the people who own the data, which is the same standard I hold product teams to.

Accessibility statement

This site is built to WCAG 2.2 Level AA. That is a deliberate professional statement, not a compliance checkbox: I believe accessibility competence belongs in the product management skill set, and gate #5 above applies to my own work first.

  • Semantic HTML landmarks and heading hierarchy throughout; a skip-to-content link is the first focusable element.
  • All text meets or exceeds a 4.5:1 contrast ratio in both light and dark themes (the page follows your system preference).
  • Fully keyboard-operable; visible focus indicators with 3px outlines and offset, never obscured by sticky elements.
  • Honors prefers-reduced-motion; no animation, autoplay, or parasitic movement.
  • No JavaScript required — the page is fully functional with scripts disabled. Disclosure widgets use native, accessible elements.
  • Text scales to 200% without loss of content or function; layout is responsive to 320px viewports.
  • No external resources: no CDN scripts, no third-party fonts, no trackers, no cookies.

If you encounter an accessibility barrier on this page, email me and I will fix it — that is also how the gates work.
